ADJECTIVES, ADVERBS, CONJUNCTIONS, AND PREPOSITIONS

Lesson 33: Adverbs (pp. 148–151)

An adverb is a word that describes a verb, an adjective, or another adverb. Most adverbs tell how, when, or where an action takes place. Many adverbs end in -ly. Adverbs can come before or after the words they describe.

Underline the adverb in each sentence. Then underline the word that adverb describes twice.

  • Alicia smiled weakly.
  • The play went smoothly until the last scene.
  • That screeching has started again.
  • Finally, the Zimmermans have left!
  • Mexico City’s climate is usually ideal.
  • The two cats wandered aimlessly from yard to yard.
  • That magazine article was quite informative.
  • The summer rain drummed noisily on the steel roof.
  • The Warners had parked nearby.
  • Luanne’s hat looked simply ridiculous.
  • For some reason, Mrs. Chen had come early.
  • Both problems were very difficult for us.
  • The flood water rose steadily.
  • Today I’ll finish my report for Mrs. Juniatta.
  • Amy can kick the ball well.
