ADJECTIVES, ADVERBS, CONJUNCTIONS, AND PREPOSITIONS

Lesson 34: Comparing with Adverbs (pp. 152–155)

An adverb can compare two or more actions. Use the correct form of an adverb when you compare. When an adverb is a one-syllable word, add -er or -est to compare. When the adverb is a word with two or more syllables, use the word more or most for comparing. Adverbs ending in -ly also use the word more or most for comparing. When comparing, either add -er or -est to the adverb, or use more or most. Do no not use more or most with -er or -est.

Write the form of the adverb in parentheses that correctly completes each sentence.

  • This year’s vacation lasted longer than last year’s. (long)
  • Linda uses chopsticks more awkwardly than Robert does. (awkwardly)
  • I finish first more often than Norman does. (often)
  • The letter carrier arrives later on Saturday than Friday. (late )
  • Men become politicians more frequently than women do. (frequently)
  • Of the eight horses, Flyaway jumped most gracefully. (gracefully)
  • Of all American poets, Anne Bradstreet was published earliest. (early)
  • The old burro moved more slowly than the old miner. (slowly)
  • Jays imitate sounds more skillfully than crows do. (skillfully)
  • Today the airplane is flying lower than yesterday. (low)
  • Of all the guesses, this one comes closest to the mark. (close).
  • Reptiles feel the cold more sharply than mammals. (sharply)
  • Which player on the soccer team practices hardest? (hard)
  • The jungle plants hid me more completely than the darkness of night. (completely)
  • We studied dolphins, whales, and porpoises, and dolphins learn most quickly. (quickly)
