ADJECTIVES AND ADVERBS

Lesson 32: Comparing with Adverbs (pp. 144–147)

An adverb is a word that generally describes an action verb. It can tell how, when, or where an action happens. You can use adverbs to compare two or more actions. Add -er to most one-syllable adverbs to compare two actions. Add -est to most one-syllable adverbs to compare more than two actions.

Write the adverb in ( ) that correctly completes each sentence.

  • Toni tried than she did before. (harder, hardest)
  • Dad laughs of anyone in the family. (louder, loudest)
  • The beets cooked than the potatoes. (quicker, quickest)
  • Of all the plants, the bean plant grew . (taller, tallest)
  • Sarah filled her basket than I did. (slower, slowest)
  • Of all the guests, we arrived . (earlier, earliest)
  • Joe felt of all when he was fishing. (happier, happiest)
  • Of all the exercises I do, jumping rope makes me breathe . (harder, hardest)
  • The orange kitten purrs of all the kittens in the litter. (softer, softest)
  • The black duckling swims to its mother than the other ducklings. (closer, closest)
  • Our team scored of all the teams. (lower, lowest)
  • Because we were late today, we walked than we usually walk. (faster, fastest)
  • Marty climbs of all the boys in the class. (higher, highest)
  • My brother writes than my sister. (neater, neatest)
  • On the weekend, I sleep than I do during the week. (longer, longest)
