Lesson 51: More Commas (pp. 228–231)

Use a comma to set off introductory words such as yes, no, or well. Use a comma after an introductory word that shows mild feeling. A word that shows feeling or emotion is an interjection. Use a comma to set off the name of a person being spoken to or addressed. The comma comes before, after, or both before and after a noun of direct address. Use a comma after an introductory prepositional phrase.

Read each sentence. Add commas where they are needed.
  • Reggie, will you help me with these suitcases?
  • Yes, I do like extra cheese on pizza, Mrs. Androtti.
  • Wow, you did a great job on the science project!
  • At Kitty Hawk, the Wright brothers flew three gliders.
  • Hold on a minute, Gerry, and I’ll ask her.
  • No, you must not feed the elephants.
  • Well, what do you think about that?
  • On April 15, the taxes must be mailed, Dave.
  • Hooray, our team has finally won a game!
  • In my opinion, we should always help our neighbors.
  • No, you may not stay at Pat’s for dinner.
  • With all our help, the program should have gone more smoothly.
  • Tell me, Richard, about your social studies test.
  • Ooh, it’s freezing in here!
  • While vacationing in Montreal, Allison practiced her French.
