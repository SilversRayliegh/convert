Lesson 44: Relative Pronouns and Relative Adverbs (pp. 196–199)

A complex sentence is made up of two related ideas. In a complex sentence, a relative pronoun introduces the second idea when it is adding information about or describing the noun in the first idea. Who, which, that, whose, and whom are relative pronouns. A relative adverb tells where, when, or why.

Write the relative pronoun or relative adverb in parentheses that correctly completes each sentence.
  • A rake, a watering can, and a shovel are the tools that we need to garden. (who, that)
  • The girl whose birthday we are celebrating is eleven years old. (that, whose)
  • I will park the car where you tell me. (where, why)
  • Is that the teacher to whom you gave the paper? (which, whom)
  • We will decorate the cake when it is done baking. (when, where)
  • Susan said "Zhen bang," which means awesome in Chinese. (who, which)
  • The reason why you should not let go of your balloon is because it will fly away. (why, when)
  • Dark clouds usually appear when a storm is coming. (why, when)
  • The grandmother who lives in Texas is his mother’s mother. (whose, who)
  • Put the coins in a safe place where you will remember them. (why, where)
  • They are trying to help the man whose car broke down. (which, whose)
  • The bird that is singing is a canary. (who, that)
  • Is that the coach whose team you are joining? (whom, whose)
  • I was the one whom he told about the festival. (which, whom)
  • This is the treehouse where the children like to play. (where, when)
