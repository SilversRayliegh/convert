NOUNS

Lesson 14: Appositives (pp. 64–67)

An appositive is a word or phrase that identifies or explains a noun. Appositives follow the nouns they identify or explain. Commas set off an appositive from the rest of the sentence.

In each sentence, underline all the words in the appositive.

  • Ms. Bell, our science teacher, will retire next year.
  • Our best pitcher, Billy Nuñez, hurt his arm.
  • Jogging, a healthy exercise, is also fun.
  • Linda showed me the present, a new game.
  • Our new pet, a fluffy kitten, is so much fun!
  • The weather, five days of rain, ruined our vacation.
  • I wrote about Thomas Jefferson, our third president.
  • The autumn, especially October, is my favorite time of year.
  • This is a sonnet, a poem with fourteen lines.
  • Sue Tong, our old friend, now lives in Paris.
  • The woman playing the organ is Mrs. Pearlman, Helen’s step-mom.
  • There is a classic old car, a 1939 DeSoto!
  • Francis Scott Key, a lawyer and poet, wrote the national anthem.
  • Icebergs, huge chunks of floating ice, have sunk many ships.
  • The White House, the home of the president, has 132 rooms.
