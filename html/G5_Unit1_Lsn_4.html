SENTENCES

Lessson 4: Subjects in Imperative Sentences (pp. 20–23)

The subject of a sentence tells whom or what the sentence is about.
An imperative sentence gives a command or makes a request. The subject of an imperative sentence is always you. The word you is not stated directly, but it is understood to be the subject.

Write the simple subject of each sentence. If the sentence is an imperative, write You.

  • Measure the number of miles to Houston.
  • No plants grow well in this soil.
  • A cruel king ruled the land harshly.
  • Please read to me your favorite poem.
  • Amelia Earhart flew across the Atlantic in 1932.
  • A terrible tornado destroyed the house last spring.
  • Look for the key under the doormat.
  • Set your alarm clock for noon at the latest.
  • Many similar paintings hang in the museum downtown.
  • Name the most unusual character in the story.
  • Take some sandwiches for the long trip home.
  • The cat with long gray hair belongs to Allison.
  • The young actor met us at the theater.
  • A rescue helicopter flew over the beach.
  • Watch out for snakes over by the creek.
