ADJECTIVES, ADVERBS, CONJUNCTIONS, AND PREPOSITIONS

Lesson 35: Using Good and Well, Real and Very (pp. 156–159)

Good is an adjective that describes a noun. Well is usually an adverb that describes a verb. It answers the question how. Well is an adjective when it means “healthy.” Real is an adjective that describes a noun. Real means “actual.” Very is an adverb that describes an adjective or another adverb. Very means “extremely.”

Write the word in parentheses that correctly completes each sentence.

  • Shake the bottle before you use it. (good, well)
  • All of our gymnasts did at the tournament. (good, well)
  • That looks enough to eat! (good, well)
  • Nita had the flu, but she is enough now. (good, well)
  • You’ve been thoughtful to me and my family, Duane. (real, very)
  • few people can read and write three languages. (Real, Very)
  • I was never interested in watching football on TV. (real, very)
  • Uncle Jerome doesn’t feel this morning. (good, well)
  • No one knew it, but the stone was a diamond. (real, very)
  • I want a new bicycle badly. (real, very)
  • Make sure the brakes are working . (good, well)
  • Regular exercise will help you stay . (good, well)
  • Give your shoes a shine before you leave. (good, well)
  • Was that a story, or did you make it up? (real, very)
  • We are all interested to hear what happens next. (real, very)
