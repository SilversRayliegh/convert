ADJECTIVES, ADVERDS, CONJUNCTIONS, AND PREPOSITIONS

Lessson 39: Prepositional Phrases (pp. 172–175)

A prepositional phrase is a group of words that begins with a preposition and ends with its object. The object is the noun or pronoun that follows the preposition. A prepositional phrase includes a preposition, the object of the preposition, and all the words between them. When a prepositional phrase begins a sentence, use a comma to separate it from the rest of the sentence.

Underline the prepositional phrase once. Underline the object (or objects) of the preposition twice.

  • A spacecraft travels across vast distances.
  • The fossils near the entrance are dinosaur eggs.
  • The disease was carried by mosquitoes and flies.
  • My dog does not like to play in the rain.
  • The daffodils sway in the warm summer breeze.
  • Behind the little boy stood a very tall woman.
  • The French call April Fool’s Day the Day of the Fish.
  • I found poems by Carl Sandburg and Eve Merriam.
  • I borrowed these tools from Ben and her.
  • Walk carefully between the rows, Deidre.
  • The largest penguin inside the aquarium stands four feet tall.
  • I do not have a pet, but I love stories about them.
  • Something felt funny on my right foot.
  • All the bushes were on sale when we purchased them.
  • The weeding was done by Marhta and me.
